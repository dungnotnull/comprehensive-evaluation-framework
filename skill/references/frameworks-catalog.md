## 1. SWOT Analysis

**Origin:** Albert Humphrey, Stanford, 1960s  
**Best For:** Any project or business — universal applicability  
**Domain:** Business Strategy, Product, Innovation

**Dimensions:**
- **Strengths** (internal, helpful): Resources, capabilities, advantages the project already has
- **Weaknesses** (internal, harmful): Gaps, limitations, vulnerabilities from within
- **Opportunities** (external, helpful): Market trends, unmet needs, timing advantages
- **Threats** (external, harmful): Competitive pressure, regulation, macro risks

**Key Question:** Is the project leveraging internal strengths to capture external opportunities while addressing internal weaknesses before external threats materialize?

## 2. PESTLE Analysis

**Origin:** Francis Aguilar, Harvard, 1967 (ETPS); expanded to PESTLE  
**Best For:** Market entry, strategy, policy, large-scale change  
**Domain:** Business Strategy, Research/Policy, Organizational

**Dimensions:**
- **Political**: Government policy, stability, trade agreements, political risk
- **Economic**: GDP growth, inflation, interest rates, consumer purchasing power, FX
- **Social**: Demographics, culture, attitudes, education, lifestyle trends
- **Technological**: Innovation pace, automation, digital disruption, R&D investment
- **Legal**: Employment law, consumer protection, IP law, compliance requirements
- **Environmental**: Climate risk, sustainability regulation, carbon, ESG expectations

**Key Question:** Which macro factors most directly affect this project's viability over its lifecycle?

## 3. Porter's Five Forces

**Origin:** Michael Porter, Harvard Business School, 1979  
**Best For:** Market entry, competitive strategy, investment decisions  
**Domain:** Business Strategy, Innovation

**Dimensions:**
- **Threat of New Entrants**: Barriers to entry (capital, IP, network effects, brand)
- **Bargaining Power of Suppliers**: Supplier concentration, switching costs, dependency
- **Bargaining Power of Buyers**: Customer concentration, price sensitivity, alternatives
- **Threat of Substitutes**: Alternative solutions that meet the same need differently
- **Competitive Rivalry**: Number and strength of current competitors, market growth rate

**Key Question:** How attractive is the competitive environment for this project, and what structural advantages or vulnerabilities exist?

## 4. Ansoff Matrix

**Origin:** Igor Ansoff, 1957  
**Best For:** Growth strategy, product/market expansion decisions  
**Domain:** Business Strategy, Product Development

**Dimensions:**
- **Market Penetration** (existing product, existing market): Lowest risk
- **Market Development** (existing product, new market): Medium risk
- **Product Development** (new product, existing market): Medium risk
- **Diversification** (new product, new market): Highest risk

**Key Question:** What growth quadrant does this project sit in, and is the organization's risk appetite and capability aligned with it?

---

## 5. McKinsey 7S

**Origin:** McKinsey & Company — Tom Peters & Robert Waterman, 1980  
**Best For:** Organizational change, transformation readiness, culture alignment  
**Domain:** Organizational Change

**Dimensions (Hard Elements):**
- **Strategy**: Plan for competitive advantage
- **Structure**: Org chart, reporting lines, division of work
- **Systems**: Processes, IT systems, workflows

**Dimensions (Soft Elements):**
- **Shared Values**: Culture, core beliefs, mission
- **Style**: Leadership approach and management culture
- **Staff**: Talent base, headcount, skills distribution
- **Skills**: Core competencies and capabilities

**Key Question:** Are all 7 elements aligned in support of the proposed project? Which misalignment is most likely to cause failure?

## 6. Balanced Scorecard

**Origin:** Kaplan & Norton, Harvard Business Review, 1992  
**Best For:** Strategic execution, performance management, initiative tracking  
**Domain:** Business Strategy, Organizational

**Dimensions:**
- **Financial**: Revenue impact, cost control, profitability, ROI
- **Customer**: Satisfaction, retention, acquisition, market share
- **Internal Processes**: Efficiency, quality, cycle time, innovation processes
- **Learning & Growth**: Employee capability, knowledge systems, culture of improvement

**Key Question:** Does the project create balanced value across all four perspectives, or does it optimize one at the expense of others?

---

## 7. OKR Alignment Audit

**Origin:** Andy Grove, Intel; popularized by Google  
**Best For:** Evaluating if objectives and key results are well-structured  
**Domain:** Organizational, Product

**Dimensions:**
- **Objective Quality**: Inspiring, qualitative, time-bound, outcome-focused (not output-focused)
- **Key Result Measurability**: Quantitative, binary-yes/no-avoidable, baseline established
- **Cascade Alignment**: Team OKRs support company OKRs
- **Stretch Calibration**: 70% achievement expected (not 100%)
- **Review Cadence**: Weekly check-in rhythm established

**Key Question:** Will these OKRs drive meaningful progress, or are they too vague/too safe/too output-focused?

## 8. Risk Matrix

**Origin:** MIL-STD-882 (US Dept of Defense); widespread adoption  
**Best For:** Every project — universal risk assessment  
**Domain:** Risk Management

**Dimensions:**
- **Likelihood** (1–5): How probable is this risk to occur?
- **Impact** (1–5): What is the consequence if it occurs?
- **Risk Score** = Likelihood × Impact (1–25 scale)
- **Mitigation Strategy**: Avoid / Transfer / Reduce / Accept
- **Residual Risk**: Risk score after mitigation is applied

**Risk Zones:**
- 🔴 15–25: Critical — immediate action required
- 🟡 8–14: High — mitigation plan mandatory
- 🟢 1–7: Low-Medium — monitor and review

**Key Question:** What are the top 3 risks by score, and are current mitigation strategies sufficient to reduce them to acceptable levels?

---

## 9. FMEA (Failure Mode and Effects Analysis)

**Origin:** US Military Procedure MIL-P-1629, 1949; automotive/aerospace industry  
**Best For:** Engineering projects, product launches, process-critical systems  
**Domain:** Risk, Technology, Product

**Dimensions per Failure Mode:**
- **Severity (S)** 1–10: How serious is the effect on the customer/outcome?
- **Occurrence (O)** 1–10: How frequently is this failure likely to happen?
- **Detection (D)** 1–10: How difficult is it to detect before it causes impact?
- **RPN** = S × O × D (Risk Priority Number, max 1000)

**Priority:** RPN > 200 = critical priority; 100–200 = high; < 100 = monitor

## 10. Design Thinking Assessment

**Origin:** IDEO / Stanford d.school  
**Best For:** Product innovation, UX-driven projects, human-centered design  
**Domain:** Product Development, Innovation

**Dimensions:**
- **Empathize**: Depth of user research; are real pain points understood?
- **Define**: Is the problem statement specific and human-centered?
- **Ideate**: Breadth and creativity of solution space explored
- **Prototype**: Speed and fidelity of learning prototypes
- **Test**: Quality of user feedback loops; iteration frequency

**Key Question:** Is this project truly human-centered, or is it solution-first with user research as a formality?

---

## 11. Jobs-To-Be-Done (JTBD)

**Origin:** Clayton Christensen, Harvard; Tony Ulwick  
**Best For:** Product-market fit assessment, innovation, feature prioritization  
**Domain:** Product Development, Innovation

**Dimensions:**
- **Job Clarity**: Is the core "job" (user goal) clearly identified?
- **Functional Job**: The practical task to be accomplished
- **Emotional Job**: How the user wants to feel
- **Social Job**: How the user wants to be perceived
- **Job Performer**: Who specifically has this job?
- **Hiring Criteria**: What makes a solution "hired" for this job?
- **Current Alternatives**: What are users currently "hiring" instead?

---

## 12. Lean Canvas

**Origin:** Ash Maurya, adapted from Business Model Canvas (Osterwalder)  
**Best For:** Startups, new products, early-stage validation  
**Domain:** Innovation, Product

**9 Blocks:**
1. **Problem**: Top 3 problems you're solving
2. **Customer Segments**: Target customers + early adopters
3. **Unique Value Proposition**: Single, clear, compelling reason to use
4. **Solution**: Top 3 features addressing the problem
5. **Channels**: Path to customers
6. **Revenue Streams**: How you make money
7. **Cost Structure**: Fixed + variable costs
8. **Key Metrics**: Numbers that matter
9. **Unfair Advantage**: What cannot be easily copied

---

## 13. Blue Ocean Canvas

**Origin:** W. Chan Kim & Renée Mauborgne, INSEAD, 2004  
**Best For:** Innovation strategy, market creation, differentiation  
**Domain:** Innovation, Business Strategy

**Dimensions (ERRC Grid):**
- **Eliminate**: Factors the industry takes for granted that should be removed
- **Reduce**: Factors that should be reduced well below industry standard
- **Raise**: Factors that should be raised well above industry standard
- **Create**: Factors that should be created that the industry has never offered

**Key Question:** Is this project competing in an existing market (red ocean) or creating an uncontested market space (blue ocean)?

---

## 14. Theory of Change

**Origin:** Carol Weiss, 1995; widely used in development/nonprofit/policy  
**Best For:** Programs, interventions, social impact projects, policy  
**Domain:** Research/Policy

**Chain:**
```
Inputs → Activities → Outputs → Short-term Outcomes → Long-term Outcomes → Impact
```

**Dimensions:**
- **Inputs**: Resources invested (people, money, time, knowledge)
- **Activities**: What the program actually does
- **Outputs**: Direct products of activities (# trained, # reached)
- **Short-term Outcomes**: Immediate changes in knowledge, attitudes, skills
- **Long-term Outcomes**: Changes in behavior, practice, relationships
- **Impact**: Fundamental changes in conditions this project aims to affect
- **Assumptions**: What must be true for each link in the chain to hold

## 15. Kirkpatrick Model

**Origin:** Donald Kirkpatrick, 1959  
**Best For:** Training programs, learning initiatives, capability development  
**Domain:** Research/Policy, Organizational

**4 Levels:**
- **Level 1 — Reaction**: Did participants find the program valuable/engaging?
- **Level 2 — Learning**: Did participants acquire the intended knowledge/skills?
- **Level 3 — Behavior**: Are participants applying new skills in their work?
- **Level 4 — Results**: Did the program produce the intended business/social outcomes?

## 16. NPV / ROI Framework

**Origin:** Financial theory; NPV from Fisher (1930), ROI standard accounting  
**Best For:** Any investment or project with financial implications  
**Domain:** Financial

**Dimensions:**
- **Initial Investment**: Total upfront cost (capex + setup)
- **Cash Flows**: Projected revenues or savings by period
- **Discount Rate**: Risk-adjusted cost of capital (WACC or hurdle rate)
- **NPV**: Sum of discounted future cash flows minus initial investment
- **ROI**: (Net Benefit / Total Cost) × 100%
- **Payback Period**: Time until cumulative cash flows break even
- **IRR**: Internal rate of return (rate at which NPV = 0)
- **Sensitivity Analysis**: How do outcomes change if key assumptions shift ±20%?

---

## 17. Cost-Benefit Analysis

**Origin:** Jules Dupuit (1848); standard in public policy and economics  
**Best For:** Policy projects, public sector, large investments, go/no-go decisions  
**Domain:** Financial, Research/Policy

**Dimensions:**
- **Direct Costs**: Implementation, operations, maintenance
- **Indirect Costs**: Opportunity cost, externalities, transition costs
- **Direct Benefits**: Quantifiable revenue or savings
- **Indirect Benefits**: Externalities, social value, brand value
- **Benefit-Cost Ratio (BCR)**: Total Benefits / Total Costs (> 1.0 = favorable)
- **Distribution**: Who bears costs vs. who receives benefits (equity analysis)

## 18. DevSecOps Maturity Model

**Origin:** OWASP, NIST frameworks, DORA research  
**Best For:** Software development projects, IT transformations, engineering orgs  
**Domain:** Technology

**Dimensions (each scored 1–5):**
- **Culture & Collaboration**: Shared ownership of quality and security
- **CI/CD Pipeline**: Automation coverage, deployment frequency, lead time
- **Security Integration**: Security testing embedded in pipeline (shift-left)
- **Monitoring & Observability**: Real-time alerting, tracing, log aggregation
- **Incident Response**: MTTR, post-mortems, runbooks
- **Feedback Loops**: How quickly insights from production reach development

---

## 19. TOGAF ADM Review

**Origin:** The Open Group Architecture Framework  
**Best For:** Enterprise architecture, large system design, IT strategy  
**Domain:** Technology

**Phases Assessed:**
- **A — Architecture Vision**: Is the target architecture clear and stakeholder-approved?
- **B — Business Architecture**: Are business processes mapped and gap-analyzed?
- **C — Information Systems**: Data and application architecture defined?
- **D — Technology Architecture**: Infrastructure, platforms, deployment defined?
- **E — Opportunities & Solutions**: Implementation roadmap realistic?
- **F — Migration Planning**: Transition architectures planned, dependencies mapped?
- **G — Implementation Governance**: Compliance monitoring, review gates defined?

---

## 20. Kano Model

**Origin:** Noriaki Kano, Tokyo Rika University, 1984  
**Best For:** Feature/product prioritization, customer satisfaction  
**Domain:** Product Development

**Categories:**
- **Must-Be (Basic)**: Expected by users; absence causes dissatisfaction; presence not noticed
- **Performance (Satisfiers)**: More = better satisfaction; less = less satisfaction (linear)
- **Delighters (Attractive)**: Unexpected features that create delight; absence not noticed
- **Indifferent**: Neither satisfies nor dissatisfies — avoid over-investing
- **Reverse**: Features some users want removed

**Key Question:** Are investments focused on Performance and Delighter features, or is too much going to Must-Be and Indifferent categories?
