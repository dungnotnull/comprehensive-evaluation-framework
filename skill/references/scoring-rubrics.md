# Scoring Rubrics — 1–5 Scale Definitions

_Load during Phase 4 when scoring each framework dimension._

## How to Use This File

For each dimension you are scoring:
1. Read the criterion description for that dimension below
2. Match the evidence from the input to the score description
3. If evidence is ambiguous, score conservatively (lower)
4. Always cite 1–2 specific pieces of evidence that drove your score
5. Never assign a score without evidence. If no evidence exists, score 2 (gap) and note "No evidence provided"

**Universal Scale:**
| Score | Label | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | 🔴 Critical Gap | Absent or severely deficient; major risk to project success |
| 2 | 🔴 Significant Weakness | Present but inadequate; requires substantial improvement |
| 3 | 🟡 Acceptable | Meets minimum bar; adequate but not differentiating |
| 4 | 🟢 Strong | Well-developed, clear, evidence-backed |
| 5 | 🟢 Exemplary | Best practice; demonstrates clear competitive or quality advantage |

## SWOT Rubrics

### Strengths
- **5**: Three or more clearly articulated, unique, defensible strengths with quantitative evidence (market share, IP owned, exclusive partnerships, proven track record)
- **4**: Two or more strengths clearly identified with qualitative evidence; differentiated from competitors
- **3**: Strengths mentioned but generic (e.g., "experienced team", "good product") without specific evidence
- **2**: One vague strength mentioned; largely undifferentiated
- **1**: No strengths identified, or stated strengths are actually weaknesses in disguise

### Weaknesses
- **5**: Weaknesses are honestly and specifically identified with mitigation plans for each; shows self-awareness
- **4**: Key weaknesses named with some context; partial mitigation plans
- **3**: Weaknesses acknowledged in general terms; no mitigation paths
- **2**: Weaknesses minimized or deflected; only surface-level acknowledgment
- **1**: No weaknesses identified (unrealistic) or critical weaknesses ignored

### Opportunities
- **5**: Specific, time-bound, quantified opportunities identified (e.g., "$2B TAM growing at 18% CAGR", "regulation change opens new segment in Q3")
- **4**: Clear opportunities with some market evidence; realistic assessment
- **3**: Generic opportunities mentioned (e.g., "growing market", "digital transformation") without specifics
- **2**: Vague optimism without evidence; or opportunities misidentified as internal strengths
- **1**: No opportunities identified, or opportunities are actually threats

### Threats
- **5**: Specific competitive, macro, and regulatory threats identified with likelihood assessment; contingency plans stated
- **4**: Key threats named and analyzed; some contingency thinking
- **3**: General threats mentioned (e.g., "competition", "economic uncertainty") without depth
- **2**: Threats minimized or treated as unlikely without analysis
- **1**: No threats identified (unrealistic) or critical threats ignored

## PESTLE Rubrics

### Political (applies to all PESTLE factors with adaptation)
- **5**: Specific current and emerging policy/regulation identified with direct impact on project; monitoring mechanism in place
- **4**: Relevant political factors identified with clear linkage to project; some uncertainty acknowledged
- **3**: General political environment noted; linkage to project implied but not explicit
- **2**: Political factors mentioned tangentially; impact on project unclear
- **1**: No political analysis; or factor has clearly high impact but is not addressed

### Economic
- **5**: Key economic indicators (GDP growth, inflation, FX, interest rates) assessed with quantified impact on project financials
- **4**: Economic trends identified with qualitative impact analysis
- **3**: General economic context noted; limited specific impact analysis
- **2**: Economy mentioned in passing; no project-specific impact
- **1**: No economic analysis despite clear economic sensitivity

### Technological
- **5**: Specific enabling and disruptive technologies identified; technology adoption curve assessed; roadmap alignment confirmed
- **4**: Key technologies identified with clear project relevance; some future-looking analysis
- **3**: Technology trends noted generally; limited specificity
- **2**: Technology mentioned without impact analysis
- **1**: No technology assessment; or rapidly changing tech context is ignored

## Porter's Five Forces Rubrics

_Note: High threat/power = low score (unfavorable for project). Low threat = high score._

### Threat of New Entrants
- **5**: Very high barriers — capital intensity, strong network effects, proprietary IP, regulation, brand; new entry effectively blocked
- **4**: Strong barriers on at least 2 dimensions; new entry difficult and slow
- **3**: Moderate barriers; new entrants possible within 1–2 years
- **2**: Low barriers; new entrants could emerge quickly and cheaply
- **1**: No meaningful barriers; market is easily entered by well-funded competitors

### Bargaining Power of Buyers
- **5**: Buyers are fragmented, price-inelastic, high switching costs; project holds strong pricing power
- **4**: Buyers have some power but are largely dependent on project's offering
- **3**: Balanced power; typical negotiation dynamics
- **2**: Buyers are concentrated or have strong alternatives; pricing pressure high
- **1**: One or a few powerful buyers dominate; price-taking environment

### Competitive Rivalry
- **5**: Market is growing fast, few competitors, high differentiation; rivalry is low
- **4**: Manageable rivalry; project has clear differentiated position
- **3**: Moderate rivalry; project competes on some dimensions with others
- **2**: Intense rivalry; many competitors, price competition, slow growth
- **1**: Cutthroat competition; commodity market, price wars, low margins

## McKinsey 7S Rubrics

### Strategy
- **5**: Strategy is clearly articulated, differentiated, and explicitly linked to the project; all trade-offs are explicit
- **4**: Strategy exists and is broadly understood; linked to project
- **3**: Strategy is present but generic; linkage to project is assumed not stated
- **2**: Strategy is vague or absent at the unit level relevant to this project
- **1**: No coherent strategy; or strategy contradicts the project's direction

### Structure
- **5**: Organizational structure explicitly designed to support this project; clear ownership, reporting lines, decision rights
- **4**: Structure is largely supportive; minor adjustments may be needed
- **3**: Existing structure can accommodate project with workarounds
- **2**: Structure creates significant friction; matrix confusion or unclear ownership
- **1**: Structure is fundamentally misaligned; reorganization would be needed for success

### Systems
- **5**: All required systems (IT, process, data) exist and are fit for purpose; integration is seamless
- **4**: Core systems exist; minor gaps addressable within project scope
- **3**: Systems partially fit; manual workarounds needed for some processes
- **2**: Significant system gaps; major implementation effort required
- **1**: Critical systems absent or incompatible; systemic replacement needed

### Shared Values
- **5**: Culture actively reinforces the project's goals; visible leadership modeling; values explicitly aligned
- **4**: Culture is generally supportive; some pockets of resistance manageable
- **3**: Culture is neutral; project is neither helped nor hindered by values
- **2**: Cultural tensions exist; significant change management effort required
- **1**: Culture actively opposes the project; deeply rooted resistance

### Skills
- **5**: All required skills exist within the team with demonstrated track record
- **4**: Most required skills present; gaps addressable through training or targeted hire
- **3**: Some key skills present; moderate skill gap requiring investment
- **2**: Significant skill gaps; project would require substantial capability building
- **1**: Critical skills absent; project is unlikely to succeed with current team

## Risk Matrix Rubrics

### Likelihood (1–5)
- **1**: Remote — less than 5% probability in project lifetime
- **2**: Unlikely — 5–25% probability
- **3**: Possible — 25–50% probability
- **4**: Likely — 50–75% probability
- **5**: Almost Certain — > 75% probability

### Impact (1–5)
- **1**: Negligible — minor inconvenience; no material effect on outcomes
- **2**: Minor — small cost or delay; easily recovered
- **3**: Moderate — noticeable effect on cost, timeline, or quality; recoverable
- **4**: Major — significant cost overrun, missed milestone, or quality failure; difficult to recover
- **5**: Catastrophic — project failure, major financial loss, reputational damage, or safety/legal consequence

## Theory of Change Rubrics

### Inputs
- **5**: All required resources are committed, funded, and allocated with specificity (who, how much, when)
- **4**: Key resources identified and largely committed; minor gaps
- **3**: Resources planned but not fully committed; some uncertainty
- **2**: Resource needs identified but significant funding or capacity gaps exist
- **1**: Resources not analyzed; or clearly insufficient for stated activities

### Activities
- **5**: Activities are specific, sequenced, time-bound, and linked to outcomes; implementation plan exists
- **4**: Activities clearly defined; timeline mostly clear
- **3**: Activities described but not sequenced; implementation approach vague
- **2**: Activities mentioned but high-level and disconnected from outcomes
- **1**: Activities absent or extremely vague; no implementation thinking

### Outputs
- **5**: Outputs are specific, countable, and directly linked to activities; measurement plan exists
- **4**: Outputs are specific and measurable; minor gaps in measurement plan
- **3**: Outputs described but not all are measurable; some counting metrics
- **2**: Outputs described in activity terms not output terms; hard to measure
- **1**: No outputs defined; or outputs are actually outcomes (chain is broken)

### Short-term Outcomes
- **5**: Specific, measurable changes expected within 12 months; baseline and target defined; attribution logic clear
- **4**: Clear outcomes with most being measurable; some baseline data
- **3**: Outcomes stated but some are vague; measurement approach unclear
- **2**: Outcomes blend with outputs; hard to distinguish changes in people/systems
- **1**: No short-term outcomes; or outcomes are actually long-term impacts (unrealistic timeline)

### Long-term Outcomes / Impact
- **5**: Ambitious but plausible; theory of how activities → outcomes → impact is explicit; assumptions stated
- **4**: Impact articulated; linkage is logical; key assumptions identified
- **3**: Impact described but linkage to activities is assumed, not demonstrated
- **2**: Impact very ambitious relative to inputs/activities (attribution gap)
- **1**: Impact claims are unrealistic or unmeasurable; no theory linking inputs to change

## Financial Framework Rubrics

### NPV / ROI Score
- **5**: NPV strongly positive (> 2× investment) with conservative assumptions; BCR > 2.0; payback < 2 years
- **4**: NPV positive under base case; BCR > 1.3; payback within project horizon
- **3**: NPV marginally positive; sensitive to key assumptions; BCR 1.0–1.3
- **2**: NPV negative under base case but positive under optimistic scenario only
- **1**: NPV negative under all scenarios; BCR < 1.0; project destroys value

### Revenue / Benefit Projections
- **5**: Bottom-up projections from validated assumptions; multiple data sources; comparable project benchmarks
- **4**: Reasonable projections with clear basis; key assumptions documented
- **3**: Projections present but basis is thin; single-point estimates without range
- **2**: Projections appear optimistic without clear basis; top-down only
- **1**: No financial projections; or projections are implausible

### Cost Analysis
- **5**: All cost categories identified (capex, opex, transition, opportunity cost); validated against benchmarks
- **4**: Major cost categories covered; some secondary costs may be missing
- **3**: High-level cost estimate; obvious categories present; hidden costs likely
- **2**: Costs significantly underestimated or missing major categories
- **1**: No cost analysis; or costs ignored entirely

## Design Thinking Rubrics

### Empathize
- **5**: Primary user research conducted (interviews, ethnography, diary studies); minimum 10+ users; jobs/pains/gains documented
- **4**: Some user research (5–9 users); clear pain point documentation
- **3**: Secondary research or small user sample (< 5); research is thin
- **2**: Assumptions about users made without research; "we know what users want"
- **1**: No user research; or solution was designed before any user input

### Define
- **5**: Problem statement is specific, human-centered, non-solution-prescribing, and validated with users
- **4**: Problem statement is clear and mostly human-centered
- **3**: Problem statement exists but is solution-adjacent or too broad
- **2**: Problem is defined as a solution ("we need a feature that..."); how might we is absent
- **1**: No problem statement; jumping straight to solution

### Ideate
- **5**: Divergent ideation documented (10+ concepts generated); convergent process used to select; diverse team involved
- **4**: Multiple concepts explored; selection rationale documented
- **3**: Some exploration but limited; fewer than 5 concepts
- **2**: One or two solutions considered; limited divergence
- **1**: Single solution assumed; no ideation process

## OKR Quality Rubrics

### Objective Quality
- **5**: Objective is inspiring, qualitative, time-bound, team-motivating, and outcome-focused (not "launch X" but "become the #1 choice for Y")
- **4**: Objective is outcome-focused and mostly inspiring; minor improvement possible
- **3**: Objective is a project milestone or output ("deliver X by Q2"); not truly outcome-focused
- **2**: Objective is a task ("implement Y"); no outcome orientation
- **1**: Objective is missing, trivial, or purely financial without strategic context

### Key Result Measurability
- **5**: All KRs have: specific metric, current baseline, target value, measurement method, and measurement frequency
- **4**: Most KRs are measurable; 1–2 may lack baseline or measurement method
- **3**: KRs are partially measurable; some are milestones rather than metrics
- **2**: KRs are mostly output-based ("complete X, deliver Y") not outcome-based
- **1**: KRs are not measurable; no metrics defined

### Stretch Calibration
- **5**: OKRs set at 70% confidence level; team confirms they're ambitious but achievable; not sandbagged
- **4**: OKRs are ambitious; slight concern about being too safe or too aggressive
- **3**: OKRs appear achievable at 90%+ confidence — too safe; no real stretch
- **2**: OKRs are clearly impossible given resources; demoralizing
- **1**: No calibration thinking; targets set arbitrarily
